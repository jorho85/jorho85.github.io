Exploring Quadratic Functions

A quadratic function is a fundamental mathematical function that plays a significant role in various fields. It is defined by the general form:

\(f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\)

Quadratic functions have a degree of 2, and they produce a curve known as a parabola when graphed.

Let's discover the versatility of quadratic functions and explore some of their many real-world applications:

  • 1. Physics: Quadratic functions describe the motion of objects under the influence of gravity, such as projectiles and falling objects.
  • 2. Engineering: Quadratic equations are used in designing bridges and structures to analyze forces and stresses.
  • 3. Economics: Quadratic functions model cost, revenue, and profit in business and economics.
  • 4. Geometry: Quadratic equations are used to solve problems related to shapes, areas, and volumes.
  • 5. Computer Graphics: Quadratic functions help create realistic 3D graphics and animations.
  • 6. Natural Sciences: Quadratic equations are applied in modeling physical and biological phenomena.

The Vertex Form: \(f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k\)

In the vertex form of a quadratic function, \(h\) represents the horizontal shift or displacement of the vertex along the \(x\)-axis. It determines the horizontal position of the vertex. $h = \frac{-b}{2a}$

To find the vertical position of the vertex (\(k\)), we can simply plug the \(h\) value into the function.

So, if \(h\) is the \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex, then \(k = f(h)\) represents the \(y\)-coordinate of the vertex.

For example, if \(f(x) = 2(x - 3)^2 + 5\), then \(h = 3\) tells us that the vertex is horizontally shifted 3 units to the right. To find \(k\), we calculate \(k = f(3) = 2(3 - 3)^2 + 5 = 5\).

The Importance of Finding Zeros of Quadratic Functions

Finding the zeros (also known as roots or x-intercepts) of quadratic functions is a fundamental concept in algebra and has significant practical importance. Here's why it matters:

  • 1. Solving Equations: Quadratic equations often arise in real-world problems. Finding the zeros helps us solve these equations and determine when certain conditions are met.
  • 2. Graphical Analysis: Zeros correspond to the points where the graph of the quadratic function intersects the x-axis. This information is vital for understanding the behavior of the function.
  • 3. Real-World Applications: Zeros represent important values in various fields such as physics, engineering, economics, and biology. For example, they help find the time when a projectile hits the ground or when a business breaks even.
  • 4. Factoring and Simplification: Zeros are essential for factoring quadratic expressions and simplifying complex expressions. This simplification can lead to more manageable forms for further analysis.
  • 5. Geometry and Quadratic Forms: Zeros are key in geometry when dealing with parabolas and quadratic equations that describe shapes, areas, and volumes.
  • 6. Optimization: In optimization problems, finding the zeros helps determine the maximum or minimum values of quadratic functions, which is valuable for making optimal decisions.

Solving Quadratic Equations by Completing the Square

When you have a quadratic equation in the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\), you can solve it by completing the square. Here's a step-by-step process:

1. Make sure the coefficient of \(x^2\) (\(a\)) is 1. If it's not, divide the entire equation by \(a\) to make it equal to 1.

2. Move the constant term (\(c\)) to the other side of the equation:

\[x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x = -c\]

3. Take half of the coefficient of \(x\) (\(b/2\)) and square it (\((b/2)^2\)). Add and subtract this value inside the parentheses:

\[ax^2 + bx + \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = -c\]

4. Factor the perfect square trinomial on the left side:

\[a\left(x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2\right) - \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = -c\]

5. Write the perfect square trinomial as \((x + \frac{b}{2a})^2\):

\[a\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = -c\]

6. Isolate the perfect square term on the left side:

\[a\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 - c\]

7. Take the square root of both sides:

\[x + \frac{b}{2a} = \pm \sqrt{\frac{b^2}{4a^2} - \frac{c}{a}}\]

8. Isolate \(x\):

\[x = -\frac{b}{2a} \pm \sqrt{\frac{b^2}{4a^2} - \frac{c}{a}}\]

These are the solutions to the quadratic equation \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) obtained by completing the square.

While the quadratic formula and graphing are the easy way to find zero's, they are typically numerical approxmations of the real answer. The only way to get the exact answer would be to use a factoring method or reduce the results of the quadratic formula by hand or with algebra software.

Reducing the Expression: \(\frac{60 + \sqrt{100}}{30}\)

We can simplify the given expression by breaking it down into smaller steps:

Step 1: Recognize that \(\sqrt{100} = 10\).

So, the expression becomes: \(\frac{60 + 10}{30}\).

Step 2: Simplify the numerator by adding \(60\) and \(10\):

\(\frac{70}{30}\).

Step 3: Reduce the fraction by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor (in this case, \(10\)):

\(\frac{7}{3}\).

So, the simplified form of the expression \(\frac{60 + \sqrt{100}}{30}\) is \(\frac{7}{3}\).

Rocket Science

We saw earlier that one of the aplications of was tracking projectiles. Quadratic functions model the behavior of a projectile acting under Newtonian gravity. Here is an example:

NASA launches a rocket at $t = 0$ seconds. Its height, in meters above sea - level, as a function of time is given by $h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 37t + 409$

Can we use that function to find out how long the rocket was in flight?

Can we use the function to find out the maximum height of the rocket?

Finding the Landing Time and Maximum Height

We have a rocket launched by NASA with a height function \(h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 37t + 409\), where \(t\) is time in seconds and \(h(t)\) is the height above sea level in meters.

To find when the rocket lands and its maximum height, we need to solve for \(t\) when \(h(t) = 0\):

1. Set \(h(t) = 0\):

\(-4.9t^2 + 37t + 409 = 0\).

2. Use the quadratic formula \(t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\) to solve for \(t\), where \(a = -4.9\), \(b = 37\), and \(c = 409\):

\[t = \frac{-37 \pm \sqrt{37^2 - 4(-4.9)(409)}}{2(-4.9)}\]

Calculating this expression will give us the time(s) when the rocket lands.

3. To find the maximum height, we can use the formula for the vertex of a parabola: \(h_{\text{max}} = -\frac{b}{2a}\).

Plug in the values of \(a\) and \(b\) from the original equation:

\[h_{\text{max}} = -\frac{37}{2(-4.9)}\]

Now, calculate \(h_{\text{max}}\) to find the maximum height reached by the rocket.
